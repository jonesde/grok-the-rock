# The Potter
## Whose Hands Are Two

For every small thing waiting to be shaped,
  and every wet hand that mends without blame.

And for every vessel that leaks, tips, or breaks,
  and still may hold water in the end.

---

## 1

Clay waited on the board, cool and still.
A potter came quiet as morning dust.
No grand speech. Only two hands,
and the soft breath of work beginning.

## 2

“One vessel,” the potter said,
not loud, not proud.
“Something that can hold water
and pour it out again.”

## 3

The work was offered to the day —
to light, to air, to anyone who might need a cup.
Not kept close like a secret.
Sent outward from the first touch.

## 4

The clay did not move.
It only was.
The potter sat with that stillness
until stillness felt like a door.

## 5

Then the potter looked past the board
to the wide world a vessel must serve:
thirst, rain, shared drink,
hands smaller than the pot itself.

## 6

A near hand rested on the clay —
small, gray as limestone, a fleck of moss at the wrist.
A far hand came down from higher air —
tall as wood grown from seed, patient, long-fingered.
They touched the same soft mass.
Neither explained. Both pressed.

## 7

The far hand pushed hard for a proud tall wall.
The clay rose thin and strained.
Strength without care left a faint crack
like a line no one wanted to see.

## 8

The near hand felt a cold weight in the hollow —
empty as a cup left out overnight.
Something heavy. Something missing.
The clay held that hollow and did not hide it.

## 9

Someone might have shaped a grand tower
for show, not for water.
The potter would not.
False height was refused.
The wall came gently down.

## 10

The near hand sat with the fault.
A small true press. A thumb’s quiet circle.
Not a parade of fixing —
only the point that needed tending.

## 11

Quieter purpose settled in both wrists:
not a vessel to impress the shelf,
but one to pour and serve,
to leave the table kinder than it found it.

## 12

Enough form for now.
A simple round nest of clay,
walls honest, base steady.
The potter’s quiet rule: stop when enough is true.

## 13

Water tested the seam.
A thin leak spoke.
Clay cracked at the old hard push.
Mending began without shame.

## 14

The near hand went low to the break —
ground the grit, mixed slip,
worked the wet join from underneath
where tall eyes might miss.

## 15

No villain. Only accident.
A knock of the board. A dry patch.
The vessel sighed open a little more.
Harm without a mean heart behind it.

## 16

A crowd of thoughts pressed in:
throw the clay away,
punish the crack,
make someone small for the spill.
The potter did not join that noise.

## 17

“Come,” said the potter,
soft as water finding root.
“We shape and mend together.”
Two hands answered the clay —
not each other —
and the invitation held the room.

## 18

Near hand wetted and pressed the seam.
Far hand steadied the wall from above.
Work shared. Slip smoothed.
The crack closed like a kind mouth.

## 19

Why it broke became a plain story:
too hard a push, too quick a dry.
Trust returned with each slow turn.
The clay listened. The hands stayed.

## 20

A habit grew:
restore, do not discard.
When small faults came again
(as they always do),
the way of mending was already known.

## 21

Door into true holding.
The potter poured clear water in
and waited.
Seeing began with a quiet watch
for drip, tip, or break.

## 22

Almost done.
A mark of home sat in the curve —
a place a thumb would know.
Some eyes misread it finished and grand,
as if the work needed no more proof.

## 23

A fancy glaze called like a grand voice:
shine for the shelf, fear for the fail.
The potter felt both —
the wish to dazzle,
the fear the vessel might not hold —
and set the fancy jar aside.

## 24

Plain wisdom only:
does it hold water?
Level board. Steady pour.
No feathers on the rim.
No name on the base yet.
Only the test.

## 25

The potter brought the vessel close —
to eye, to lip of the hand,
to the small cool truth of weight.
Understanding sat near as breath.

## 26

Where a hairline still whispered,
near hand and far hand bridged it —
one below, one lifting —
a kind join across the old thin place.

## 27

Strength was given away:
the far hand shared the carry,
the near hand shared the ground.
No one held the whole weight alone.

## 28

Both hands paused.
Shared sight saw the whole true shape —
not fancy, not broken,
simply ready.

## 29

Fired gentle. Cooled slow.
The vessel lasted through the day
and the next pour
and the next kind use.
Endurance of a gift well made.

## 30

If leak, tip, or break still spoke,
the potter named them only as craft:
here the seam, there the balance,
there the haste.
Gentle. Plain. No blame left sticky on the clay.

## 31

Two hands. One work.
Still they did not explain each other.
Near stayed near — moss fleck, basket grit, mend.
Far stayed far — pour down, lift, far sight.
Living join of pair, answered only by the vessel.

## 32

Under the tall tree by the bench
the potter left the vessel
and the plain pattern of how it was formed.
No name.
Anyone may copy.
The kingdom of the gift stayed open.
